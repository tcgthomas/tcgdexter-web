# Knockouts and prize trading

This is the lesson that separates "I'm playing the game" from "I'm playing to win." Stay with me — once this clicks, you'll see every match differently.

## When a Pokémon gets knocked out

A Pokémon is **knocked out** the moment its damage meets or exceeds its HP. It and everything attached to it (energies, tools) go to the discard pile.

The player who landed the knockout takes a prize card from their stack of six.

## Not every knockout is worth the same

Here's the wrinkle that drives the whole game:

- A normal Pokémon gives up **1 prize card** when knocked out
- A Pokémon marked **ex** gives up **2 prize cards**
- A **Mega** Pokémon gives up **3 prize cards**

So when you attack, you're not just thinking *"can I knock this out?"* — you're thinking *"is this knockout worth the cost?"*

## The "prize trade"

A **prize trade** is the exchange of knockouts between you and your opponent over the course of a game. The math is brutally simple: you want trades that get you closer to 6 prizes faster than they get to 6.

Some examples:

- You knock out their ex with a non-ex attacker → **you take 2 prizes for 1 of yours**. Excellent trade.
- They knock out your ex with their ex → **even trade**, 2-for-2. Fine.
- They knock out your non-ex with their ex → **bad for them, good for you**. They paid an expensive Pokémon to take only 1 prize.

A skilled player will avoid using a 2-prize attacker to take a 1-prize knockout when they can help it. The whole game becomes a dance of trying to give up fewer prizes than you take.

## "Single prize" decks

This is why **single-prize decks** (decks built around only non-ex attackers) are competitive even when ex Pokémon are stronger card-for-card. If every Pokémon you put on the board is worth 1 prize, your opponent has to knock out *six* of them to win. Meanwhile, you can hit their ex Pokémon for 2 prizes each.

## How this changes deckbuilding

When you're building a deck, you're not just picking strong cards — you're picking attackers whose prize trade math works in your favor against the meta. Dexter's archetype matching helps with this, because the popular meta decks have known prize structures.
